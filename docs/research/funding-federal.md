# Federal + Rural Funding Programs for LakeLife (Indiana LLC, pre-revenue, service marketplace)

Researched live July 2026. Ranked by realistic fit — most are honest near-misses; the R&D payroll tax credit is the standout.

---

## 1. Federal R&D Payroll Tax Credit (IRC §41, using the §41(h) small-business election) — **BEST REALISTIC FIT**

- **Administering body:** IRS (claimed on Form 6765 / Form 8974, no application or approval process — it's a tax election, not a grant)
- **What it gives:** A dollar-for-dollar credit for qualifying research expenses (QREs — W-2 wages for people doing the work, 65% of contractor costs, supplies). As a pre-revenue "Qualified Small Business" (QSB — gross receipts under $5M and no gross receipts more than 5 years before the credit year), LakeLife can elect to apply the credit against its **employer share of payroll taxes (FICA/Medicare) instead of income tax** — real cash even with zero profit. OBBBA (enacted July 2025) raised the annual cap from $250K to **$500K/year**, for up to 5 years, for tax years beginning after 12/31/2025 — LakeLife's first tax year as a new LLC would qualify at the higher cap. Lifetime cap $2.5M.
- **Eligibility fit — honest:** Real, but conditional on what actually counts as "research."
  - Passes the **four-part test** (technological in nature, eliminates uncertainty, process of experimentation, permitted business purpose) for genuinely custom engineering work — e.g., building your own dispatch-matching/scheduling-optimization algorithm, dynamic pricing engine, or photo-gate/computer-vision logic from scratch.
  - **Does NOT qualify**: simply calling Google Maps Directions API, wiring up Twilio/Resend/Supabase per their docs, or standard CRUD app-building. That's routine software engineering, not R&D.
  - Because LakeLife's platform is **customer/vendor-facing** (not software built solely for internal company use), it likely avoids the stricter 3-part "internal use software" test that trips up a lot of internal tools — but any genuinely internal-only tooling (e.g., an internal ops dashboard) would need to clear that higher bar separately.
  - Founder-led dev qualifies **only if the founder is paid W-2 wages** — LLC member draws/guaranteed payments generally aren't wages for this purpose, so payroll needs to be structured accordingly (worth a conversation with a CPA about S-corp election or W-2 payroll for the technical founder).
- **Effort/cost:** Low-to-moderate. No federal application — just documentation (contemporaneous notes on what was uncertain and what was tried) and a CPA/R&D-credit specialist to prepare Form 6765. Section G (itemized business-component detail) becomes **mandatory for all filers starting tax year 2026**, though QSBs electing the payroll offset get simplified reporting.
- **Timing:** Ongoing — elected on the timely-filed original return for the tax year, applied against payroll tax returns in quarters after that return is filed. Not deadline-driven like a grant cycle, but do it right the first time (the election can't be made on an amended return).
- **Sources:** [Strike Tax 2026 guide](https://www.striketax.com/journal/r-d-tax-credits-for-startups-and-small-businesses-what-you-need-to-know), [Beancount.io payroll offset guide](https://beancount.io/blog/2026/05/01/rd-tax-credit-section-41-startup-payroll-offset-guide), [Anomaly CPA 2026 guide](https://www.anomalycpa.com/post/r-d-tax-credit-guide-for-startups-in-2026-who-qualifies-and-how-the-payroll-offset-works), [IRS Form 6765 instructions (12/2025)](https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i6765), [CBIZ Form 6765 2025 update](https://www.cbiz.com/insights/article/getting-ahead-of-the-curve-key-updates-to-research-experimental-expense-deductions-and-comprehensive-form-6765), [BDO on Form 6765 Section G transition](https://www.bdo.com/insights/tax/irs-extends-transition-period-for-research-credit-refund-claims), [internal-use-software 3-part test](https://www.striketax.com/journal/internal-use-software-and-the-r-d-tax-credit)

## 2. USDA Business & Industry (B&I) Guaranteed Loan Program — real, but it's debt

- **Administering body:** USDA Rural Development (loan originated through a private lender, USDA guarantees 80–85%)
- **What it gives:** Not a grant — a **loan guarantee** that makes a private bank more willing to lend, potentially at better terms/longer amortization. FY2026: loans under $5M get an 85% guarantee, $5M+ get 80%.
- **Eligibility fit:** Genuinely plausible. LaGrange and Steuben Counties are rural (no town over 50,000 people), so geographic eligibility is easy to clear. B&I isn't limited to manufacturing — service and technology businesses qualify, and the exclusion list (lenders/insurers, gambling, adult content, golf courses, racetracks, amusement parks, motion pictures, speculative real estate) doesn't touch a service-coordination marketplace. Usable for working capital, equipment, and even some business acquisition/expansion costs.
- **Caveat:** This is **debt with personal/business obligation to repay**, not free money — flagging per your brief's instruction to note SBA-type debt programs "briefly." Same logic applies here since it's structurally identical to an SBA loan guarantee, just through USDA.
- **Effort:** Moderate-to-high — you apply through a participating lender, not directly to USDA; requires a full loan package (financials, projections, collateral discussion).
- **Timing:** Rolling/no fixed deadline; lender-driven.
- **Sources:** [USDA B&I Guaranteed Loan program page](https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/business-programs/business-and-industry-guaranteed-loan), [eCFR 7 CFR 4279 Subpart B](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-7/subtitle-B/chapter-XLII/part-4279/subpart-B), [USDA OneRD Guarantee](https://www.rd.usda.gov/onerdguarantee)

## 3. SBA Growth Accelerator Fund Competition (GAFC) — indirect fit only

- **Administering body:** U.S. Small Business Administration
- **What it gives:** Up to $9M total in FY2026 (largest cycle in program history), individual awards $75K–$150K.
- **Eligibility fit — honest:** **LakeLife itself cannot apply.** GAFC funds go to accelerators/entrepreneur support organizations (ESOs), not directly to the startups they serve. The only path in is indirect — LakeLife could become a portfolio company of a winning accelerator in Indiana (e.g., a regional ESO) and get non-dilutive support/mentorship that way, but there's no direct-to-startup dollars here.
- **Effort:** N/A for direct application; worth watching if a NE-Indiana or statewide ESO applies and you could be named as a supported company.
- **Timing:** Historically Stage 1 opens Q1 and closes within weeks — 2026 cycle timing not independently confirmed beyond that pattern.
- **Sources:** [Congress.gov CRS brief](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/IF12310), [fi.co GAFC overview](https://fi.co/insight/the-sba-growth-accelerator-fund-is-surging-here-s-what-edos-should-know)

## 4. NSF SBIR/STTR — America's Seed Fund (NSF 26-510) — weak fit, honest reasons

- **Administering body:** National Science Foundation
- **What it gives:** Phase I up to ~$305K (statutory cap for the whole SBIR/STTR system is $314,363 in 2026); Phase II up to ~$2M+; a new $30M "Strategic Breakthrough" tier and a $40M scientific-instrumentation pilot were added in the 2026 relaunch.
- **Eligibility fit — honest:** Formally open ("all areas of science and engineering," topics are non-exhaustive examples) and the basic eligibility bar is easy to clear (for-profit US small business, <500 employees, PI works ≥20 hrs/week for the company, not majority VC/PE-owned). **But the real bar is technical/scientific risk, not product execution.** NSF SBIR wants proposals demonstrating unproven, high-risk R&D with genuine technical uncertainty — not "we're integrating mapping APIs and building a nice booking UI for lake-home service dispatch." If LakeLife's AI dispatch/routing work involves a genuinely novel algorithmic contribution (e.g., a new approach to multi-constraint scheduling optimization under uncertain lake-ice/weather windows, published-paper-worthy), there's a narrow path under an AI or "Other Topics" area. As currently scoped (applied product engineering on top of existing services), this is a stretch and reviewers will likely see it as commercialization, not research.
- **Program status note:** NSF paused new Project Pitch submissions Dec 22, 2025–June 2, 2026 during the SBIR/STTR reauthorization lapse; Congress reauthorized the program through Sept 30, 2031 in April 2026. Program is now active again with rolling Project Pitch windows (next full-proposal deadline noted as July 27, 2026, then Nov 4, 2026, March 4, 2027).
- **Effort:** High — Project Pitch (short) then, if invited, a full proposal; multi-month process even for Phase I.
- **Sources:** [NSF 26-510 solicitation](https://www.nsf.gov/funding/opportunities/small-business-innovation-research-small-business-technology/nsf26-510/solicitation), [Granted AI relaunch summary](https://grantedai.com/blog/nsf-sbir-sttr-250-million-relaunch-project-pitch-july-27-deadline-deep-tech-startups-strategy-2026), [seedfund.nsf.gov](https://seedfund.nsf.gov/solicitations/)

## 5. DOT SBIR (FY26) — missed this cycle, weak topical fit anyway

- **Administering body:** U.S. Department of Transportation (Volpe Center administers)
- **What it gives:** 10 topics across FHWA/FRA/FTA/NHTSA/PHMSA, Phase I awards $200K–$300K.
- **Eligibility fit:** Topics skew toward safety analytics, hazmat packaging, battery safety, transit planning — not consumer service-marketplace dispatch. Even the AI/logistics-adjacent topics are aimed at freight/transportation-systems research, a real stretch for a lake-home services app.
- **Timing — already closed:** FY26 Phase I solicitation ran June 3–July 7, 2026. **That window is now closed** (today is July 23, 2026). Watch for FY27.
- **Sources:** [Volpe Center FY26 SBIR notice](https://www.volpe.dot.gov/work-us/small-business-innovation-research/fy26-solicitation-now-open), [Granted AI DOT FY26 summary](https://grantedai.com/blog/dot-fy26-sbir-phase-i-june-3-july-7-deadline-ten-topics-fhwa-fra-fta-nhtsa-phmsa-volpe-strategy-2026)

## 6. SBA 7(a) / Microloans — debt, noted briefly per instructions

- **7(a):** General-purpose SBA-guaranteed bank loans; largest SBA program, works for working capital/equipment, but debt requiring personal guarantee — not a grant.
- **Microloans:** Up to $50,000 (FY2026 average actually disbursed is ~$15,400), delivered through nonprofit intermediary lenders, terms up to 7 years, rates ~8–13%. Usable for equipment/working capital; often paired with free technical assistance/business coaching from the intermediary — that coaching may be the more valuable part for a pre-revenue founder.
- **Fit:** Available in principle, but it's a loan, not free capital — same category as the USDA B&I program above, just smaller-dollar and easier to qualify for.
- **Sources:** [SBA Microloans](https://www.sba.gov/funding-programs/loans/microloans), [NerdWallet SBA microloan guide](https://www.nerdwallet.com/business/loans/learn/sba-microloans)

## Programs checked and ruled out — flagged as NOT a fit (intermediary-only structure)

These all came up prominently in searches as "rural business" money, but every one of them **excludes for-profit businesses as direct applicants** — funds flow only to nonprofits, cooperatives, universities, tribes, or local governments, who may then (at their discretion) pass benefits to businesses like LakeLife indirectly (training, TA, sub-grants to their microloan funds):

| Program | Administrator | Why it's not a direct fit |
|---|---|---|
| USDA Rural Business Development Grant (RBDG) | USDA Rural Development | For-profits explicitly ineligible to apply; goes to nonprofits/co-ops/local govt. Deadlines were June 15/30, 2026 (already passed). [Source](https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/business-programs/rural-business-development-grants) |
| USDA Rural Microentrepreneur Assistance Program (RMAP) | USDA Rural Development | Grants go to nonprofit "Microenterprise Development Organizations," who then make microloans — LakeLife would be a potential *borrower from an MDO*, not an applicant. [Source](https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/business-programs/rural-microentrepreneur-assistance-program) |
| USDA Value-Added Producer Grants (VAPG) | USDA Rural Development | Restricted to agricultural producers/producer groups adding value to farm products — no plausible fit for a service marketplace. FY26 window closed April 22, 2026. [Source](https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/business-programs/value-added-producer-grants) |
| USDA Rural Innovation Stronger Economy (RISE) grants | USDA Rural Development | Goes to regional "job accelerator partnerships" (universities, EDOs, nonprofits), $500K–$2M over 4 years, ~4 awards nationally — extremely competitive and not open to a single startup. [Source](https://www.rd.usda.gov/programs-services/business-programs/rural-innovation-stronger-economy-rise-grants) |
| EDA Build to Scale (incl. former i6 Challenge) | U.S. Economic Development Administration (Commerce) | Explicitly nonprofits/501(c)(3)s/universities/local govt only; award funds legally cannot be passed directly to participant startups. [Source](https://grantedai.com/grants/build-to-scale-b2s-programs-eda-u-s-economic-development-administration--a662c3d0) |

## Bottom line

For a bootstrapped, founder-led, pre-revenue Indiana LLC building a service-coordination marketplace, the federal/rural lane has **one real, low-effort, recurring win** (the R&D payroll tax credit — potentially tens of thousands of dollars a year in actual cash via reduced payroll tax deposits, contingent on genuinely custom technical work and clean documentation) and **one plausible-but-debt option** (USDA B&I loan guarantee, given the rural location). Everything else in this federal/rural lane is either structurally closed to for-profits (most USDA rural grants, EDA Build to Scale), a weak topical/technical-risk fit (NSF/DOT SBIR), or indirect (SBA GAFC via an accelerator). None of the direct-to-business federal grant programs I could find are a genuine match — the honest recommendation is to treat this lane as "one solid tax-credit play, skip the rest," and put more weight on your state-lane and private/founder-capital options.
