# Opportunity Zones — LaGrange & Steuben County, Indiana: Research Findings

## Bottom line up front

**Opportunity Zone tax benefits are not available to LakeLife today, and are unlikely to become available even after the 2027 redesignation round.** Neither LaGrange County nor Steuben County, Indiana currently contains a single designated Opportunity Zone census tract, and both counties are economically too healthy (low poverty, income at/above state median) to be strong candidates for the tighter OZ 2.0 eligibility tests. Separately, OZ benefits accrue to *investors* with capital gains, not to the operating company — so even a favorable designation would only matter if LakeLife restructured to solicit capital-gains money through a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF), and even then a lightly-capitalized software marketplace would face real friction under the "active conduct of business **in** the zone" test. **Realistic fit: low, and time-sensitive if you want to pursue it at all — the window that mattered most for this decade's designation has largely already closed as of today (July 23, 2026).**

## 1. Current OZ law as of mid-2026 — the OBBBA changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, restructured Opportunity Zones from a one-time, expiring TCJA provision into a **permanent** part of the tax code ("OZ 2.0"), with these mechanics current as of today:

- **Permanence + decennial redesignation.** OZs no longer expire; instead, every state's governor must redesignate zones every 10 years. [PwC](https://www.pwc.com/us/en/services/tax/library/enhanced-and-permanent-opportunity-zones-as-part-of-the-obba.html), [RSM](https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/obbba-tax-opportunity-zones.html)
- **New designation round now underway.** The nomination window opened **July 1, 2026** and runs 90 days — statutory deadline **September 28, 2026**, extendable 30 days to **October 28, 2026** at a governor's request (Rev. Proc. 2026‑12). New zones take effect **January 1, 2027** and run through **December 31, 2036**. The old 2018 zones stay in effect through an overlap period ending **December 31, 2028**. [Congress.gov CRS R48952](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48952), [ArentFox Schiff](https://www.afslaw.com/perspectives/alerts/irs-releases-rev-proc-2026-12-new-roadmap-oz-20-designations)
- **Governors may nominate up to 25%** of a state's eligible low-income tracts (down from a broader pool under 1.0, and the old "non‑low‑income contiguous tract" loophole is eliminated entirely — every nominated tract must independently pass an income/poverty test). [OpportunityZones.com](https://opportunityzones.com/2026/01/new-census-data-defines-opportunity-zone-2-0-eligible-tracts/)
- **Tightened eligibility tests** for a tract to even be nominate-able:
  - Test 1 (income only): median family income ≤ **70%** of statewide or metro-area median (down from 80% under OZ 1.0), **or**
  - Test 2 (poverty + income): poverty rate ≥ **20%** *and* median family income ≤ **125%** of area median.
  Nationally, about a quarter fewer tracts qualify than under the old rules. [OpportunityZones.com](https://opportunityzones.com/2026/01/new-census-data-defines-opportunity-zone-2-0-eligible-tracts/)
- **Investor mechanics changed too, for capital gains invested after 12/31/2026:**
  - A **rolling 5‑year deferral**: deferred gain is recognized at the earlier of sale of the QOF interest or 5 years after investment (replacing the old fixed Dec‑31‑2026 recognition date). [Godfrey & Kahn](https://www.gklaw.com/Insights/Qualified-Opportunity-Zones-Enhanced-and-Extended-Under-the-One-Big-Beautiful-Bill-Act.htm)
  - **Basis step-up at 5 years: 10%** of the deferred gain is excluded (a single step, replacing the old 10%/15% two-step schedule). [Godfrey & Kahn](https://www.gklaw.com/Insights/Qualified-Opportunity-Zones-Enhanced-and-Extended-Under-the-One-Big-Beautiful-Bill-Act.htm)
  - **10‑year hold still gives full exclusion** of appreciation on the QOF investment itself — this benefit is unchanged and now permanent. [Novogradac / OpportunityZones.com FAQ](https://opportunityzones.com/faq/what-is-the-10-year-rule-for-opportunity-zones/)
- **Enhanced rural benefits — the piece most relevant to LakeLife's geography:** OBBBA creates the **Qualified Rural Opportunity Fund (QROF)**, which must hold ≥90% of its assets in rural, low-income-community businesses/property. Investors in a QROF get a **30% basis step-up** at the 5-year mark (vs. 10% standard), and the **substantial-improvement threshold drops from 100% to 50%** of a property's basis for rural QOZ property (i.e., a rural QOZB only has to add improvements equal to half the acquisition basis, not double it). "Rural" is defined as any area **not** in a city/town of 50,000+ people — so essentially all of LaGrange and Steuben Counties would qualify as "rural" *if* they had an eligible tract. [Wiggam Law](https://wiggamlaw.com/blog/oz-obbb/), [Bernstein Shur](https://www.bernsteinshur.com/insights-events/opportunity-zones-2-0-a-new-era-for-investment-especially-in-rural-communities/), [Godfrey & Kahn](https://www.gklaw.com/Insights/Qualified-Opportunity-Zones-Enhanced-and-Extended-Under-the-One-Big-Beautiful-Bill-Act.htm)
- **New reporting burden:** QOFs must now report NAICS codes, tangible/intangible property values, tracts invested in, and employee counts semi-annually, with penalties up to $50,000/year for large funds. [Thomson Reuters](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/news/tax-experts-on-obbba-changes-to-opportunity-zones/)

## 2. Are any LaGrange or Steuben County tracts currently designated?

**No — verified. Zero designated Opportunity Zone census tracts in either county under the current (2018 "OZ 1.0") map**, which stays in effect through Dec 31, 2028:

- Steuben County: "**0** designated Qualified Opportunity Zone census tracts" — confirmed directly, county-specific page. [OpportunityZonesMap.com — Steuben County](https://opportunityzonesmap.com/states/indiana/steuben-county)
- LaGrange County: "**0** designated Qualified Opportunity Zone census tracts" — confirmed directly, county-specific page. [OpportunityZonesMap.com — LaGrange County](https://opportunityzonesmap.com/states/indiana/lagrange-county)

This is consistent with the broader picture: Indiana designated 156 tracts across only **58 of its 92 counties** in 2018 [Terre Haute Chamber of Commerce](https://www.terrehautechamber.com/2018/05/02/terre-haute-tracts-designated-opportunity-zones-by-gov-holcomb/), concentrated in metro areas — Indianapolis (36 tracts), Fort Wayne (10), Gary (8) [EIG Indiana OZ Profile PDF](https://eig.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/08/Indiana-OZ-Profile.pdf) — and neither LaGrange nor Steuben County appears on any county list I could find (opportunityzones.com's own county pages for both returned HTTP 410 Gone, but the independent opportunityzonesmap.com source gave an explicit "0" for each). **So: Wolcottville, Topeka, LaGrange town, and Angola are not, and have never been, in an Opportunity Zone.**

This also tracks the underlying economics — neither county looks like a distressed-community candidate:

| County | Poverty rate | Median household income | Indiana context |
|---|---|---|---|
| LaGrange | ~5.9% | ~$84,487 | well above IN state median (~$65–70k) |
| Steuben | ~8.8% | ~$71,483 | at/above IN state median |

[WelfareInfo.org — LaGrange](https://www.welfareinfo.org/poverty-rate/indiana/lagrange-county/), [Data USA — Steuben County](https://datausa.io/profile/geo/steuben-county-in)

Neither county-wide figure comes close to the OZ 2.0 poverty threshold (20%+) or the income threshold (≤70% of area median for income-only, or ≤125% for the poverty test). **Caveat:** these are *county-wide* averages; a specific census tract within a county can differ meaningfully from the county mean (e.g., a lower-income village pocket), and LaGrange County's large Amish population can also produce unusual ACS income reporting for specific tracts. I was not able to pull tract-by-tract ACS figures for the ~4 tracts in LaGrange County or ~9 tracts in Steuben County (Data USA surfaced Steuben tracts 9711/9712/9716 with median incomes of $80–90k, i.e. affluent lake-area tracts, but I don't have full county coverage). If you want certainty, the two live-data tools to check are the state's own [IEDC OZ Eligibility Map](https://iedc.in.gov/program/indiana-opportunity-zones/oz-eligibility-map) and [OpportunityZones.com's OZ 2.0 Eligibility Map](https://opportunityzones.com/tools/oz-2-eligibility-map/) — both are interactive/JS-rendered and I could not extract county-specific tract lists from them programmatically.

## 3. Would OZ status even help LakeLife? The QOZB tests

This is the part worth being blunt about with the founder: **Opportunity Zone tax benefits flow to investors who roll capital gains into a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) — not to LakeLife as a company.** LakeLife itself doesn't get a credit or a deduction. The only way this matters is if (a) a tract near LakeLife's operations were designated, and (b) LakeLife structured itself (or a subsidiary) as a **Qualified Opportunity Zone Business (QOZB)** that a QOF could invest equity into, so that an investor with existing capital gains (e.g., from selling a business, stock, or property) could defer/reduce tax by funding LakeLife that way.

If that setup existed, a QOZB must pass these tests (unchanged by OBBBA, per [PIDC](https://pidcphila.com/blog/qualified-opportunity-zone-business/), [McDermott](https://www.mcdermottlaw.com/insights/the-tangible-property-test/), [GBQ](https://gbq.com/qualified-opportunity-zone-for-operating-businesses/)):

- **70% tangible property test** — at least 70% of the tangible property the QOZB owns/leases must be "Qualified Opportunity Zone Business Property," located in the zone. *For a software marketplace this is actually the easy test* — LakeLife has almost no tangible property (laptops, maybe an office), and if virtually all of it sits in a leased office inside the zone, 70% is trivially met precisely because the denominator is small.
- **50% gross income test** — at least 50% of gross income must be derived from the **active conduct of business in the zone**. This has several safe harbors: 50%+ of employee/contractor *hours* worked in the zone, or 50%+ of *service payments* for work done in the zone, or a combined tangible-property-plus-management-functions test, or a facts-and-circumstances fallback. **This is the real problem for LakeLife.** LakeLife's revenue is generated by bookings and payments tied to homeowner properties on Big Long, Pretty, and Big Turkey Lakes and vendor labor performed at those properties — none of which would be "in the zone" unless the zone happened to be drawn around the lakes themselves (which it isn't, since there is no zone there at all). Having an office or dispatch hub physically located in a designated tract would help satisfy the hours/services safe harbors for that *portion* of work, but the vendor labor and on-property service delivery — the actual value LakeLife coordinates — happens outside any zone. This is a genuine structural mismatch between a geographically-dispersed service marketplace and a place-based tax incentive designed around fixed real estate/employment.
- **Working capital safe harbor** — a QOZB can hold cash for up to 31 months without it counting against the tangible-property/nonqualified-financial-property limits, provided there's a written plan spending it on qualifying tangible property/improvements/working capital in the zone. Unchanged by OBBBA. [McDermott](https://www.mcdermottlaw.com/insights/basics-of-qof-investing-what-is-a-qozb/)
- **Intangible property** — a QOZB must use a "substantial portion" (40%) of its intangible property (which for a software company like LakeLife would be most of its value — the platform, IP, brand) in the active conduct of business **within the zone**. Again, this cuts against a business whose whole point is coordinating activity across multiple lake properties outside any zone.
- **No "sin business."** Not relevant to LakeLife (gambling, liquor stores, massage parlors, etc. are excluded; a service marketplace isn't).

**Net assessment:** even setting aside that no zone currently exists in LakeLife's service area, a coordination/marketplace platform is a structurally awkward fit for QOZB status — its tangible-property test is easy, but its income-and-intangible-property tests assume the *value-generating activity* happens inside a fixed geographic tract, which doesn't describe a platform dispatching vendors to homeowner lake properties spread across two counties.

## 4. Could 2027 rural designations plausibly cover LaGrange or Steuben County, and what would positioning now look like?

**Plausibility:** Low-to-moderate, and I could not verify a specific eligible tract exists in either county — but given the tightened OZ 2.0 income/poverty tests and the county-wide affluence shown above, I would not bet on it without tract-level confirmation. It's not impossible (rural counties can have one pocket-poverty tract even when the county average looks healthy), but nothing I found flags a specific eligible tract near Wolcottville, Topeka, LaGrange town, Angola, Fremont, or Orland.

**Timing — this is the important, time-sensitive finding.** Indiana's own process (via IHCDA) for this cycle is:

| Phase | Window |
|---|---|
| IHCDA drafts nomination form | Apr 1 – May 14, 2026 |
| **Local government / county / LEDO submission portal open** | **May 15 – June 30, 2026** |
| Governor's preliminary selections | July 1 – July 31, 2026 |
| Public comment on preliminary list | Aug 1 – Aug 15, 2026 |
| Governor's final selections → submitted to Treasury | Aug 16 – Aug 30, 2026 |
| Federal statutory deadline (extendable +30 days) | Sept 28, 2026 (→ Oct 28 max) |
| OZ 2.0 effective | Jan 1, 2027, for 10 years |

[IHCDA — Opportunity Zones 2.0](https://www.in.gov/ihcda/program-partners/oz-2.0/)

**As of today (July 23, 2026), the formal window for LaGrange County, Steuben County, or their local economic development corporations to submit a nomination has already closed** (the portal ran May 15–June 30; one press account also referenced a general public input window through July 17). The state is currently inside the Governor's "preliminary selections" phase. Since designations only happen once per decade going forward, **missing this round means the next opportunity isn't until roughly 2036.**

**Realistic positioning now, if you want to pursue this at all:**
1. **Call LaGrange County EDC** (513 W. Central Ave, LaGrange, IN 46761; (260) 499‑4994; CEO Sherri Johnston) and **Steuben County EDC** (steubenedc.com) this week and ask two things: did they submit a tract nomination in the May–June window, and is there any avenue to flag a tract during the **Aug 1–15, 2026 public comment period** on the Governor's preliminary list. Only the Governor has statutory authority to submit to Treasury (IRC §1400Z‑1) — IHCDA's local portal was Indiana's internal input mechanism, not a separate legal channel — so public comment on the preliminary list is genuinely the last lever before Aug 30.
2. **Don't build any near-term plans around this.** Given the effort required (advocacy through a closing state process, on a speculative tract, for a benefit that flows to investors rather than the company, for a business model that doesn't cleanly satisfy the QOZB income test even if a zone existed) versus the payoff, this ranks as **low priority** relative to whatever other incentive programs are being evaluated in the broader LakeLife funding research. It's worth a two-phone-call check this week and then deprioritizing.
